  \begin{block}{Manifolds and complex dimensions}

    A \auterm{manifold} is a mathematician's word for a smooth shape --- of any
    dimension. Its one defining trick: up close it looks \textbf{flat}, the way
    the ground looks flat from where you stand even though the Earth is round.
    The number of coordinates needed to pin down a point is the
    \textbf{dimension}; the shapes here are \textbf{compact} --- finite and
    edgeless, closed up like a sphere rather than sprawling like an open plain.

    \auterm{Complex coordinates.}\ Calabi--Yau manifolds run on \emph{complex}
    coordinates. A complex number $x+iy$ is a single arrow in the plane ---
    length and direction bundled into one --- so one complex dimension already
    carries two real ones. An $n$-complex-dimensional shape is
    $2n$-real-dimensional: a Calabi--Yau \textbf{threefold} $(n=3)$ is a
    \textbf{six}-dimensional world.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Curvature: do triangles behave?}

    How can you tell a surface is curved without stepping outside it? Draw a
    triangle. On a \textbf{sphere} its angles sum to \textbf{more} than
    $180^\circ$; on a \textbf{saddle}, to \textbf{less}; only on a
    \textbf{flat} sheet do they hit $180^\circ$ exactly. An ant that never
    leaves the surface could discover its world is round this way ---
    curvature is \textbf{intrinsic}, and it can change from point to point.

    \begin{figure}
      \centering
      \includegraphics[width=0.92\colwidth]{figures/curvature-trio.png}
      \caption{Sphere: more than $180^\circ$ \,$\cdot$\, flat sheet: exactly
        $180^\circ$ \,$\cdot$\, saddle: less than $180^\circ$.}
    \end{figure}

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Ricci curvature}

    Past two dimensions, curvature splits into many independent pieces. The
    \auterm{Ricci curvature} is the piece that controls \textbf{volume}.
    Sprinkle a tiny ball of dust and let each speck drift along a straight
    line: on a sphere the ball shrinks, on a saddle it swells, and when
    $\operatorname{Ric}=0$ it holds its volume exactly. Such a space is
    genuinely curved yet volume-balanced --- which is precisely Einstein's
    equation for a spacetime with no matter. \textbf{Ricci-flat spaces are
    gravity's vacuums.}

  \end{block}

\end{column}

  \begin{block}{What is a Calabi--Yau manifold?}

    A \auterm{Calabi--Yau manifold} is a compact complex manifold with two
    features. \auterm{(1) K\"ahler:}\ it carries both a way to measure
    distance --- a metric --- and complex coordinates, fitting together as
    smoothly as possible. \auterm{(2) A holomorphic volume form} $\Omega$:\
    a way to gauge complex volume, built from the coordinates, that vanishes
    \emph{nowhere} (in bundle language, a trivial canonical bundle
    $K_X \cong \mathcal{O}_X$). Feature (2) forces a purely
    \textbf{topological} quantity to vanish --- the \auterm{first Chern class}
    $c_1(X)$, a single whole-number invariant of the shape you can compute
    without measuring anything. Calabi asked whether that cheap test forces a
    Ricci-flat metric to exist.

  \end{block}

  \begin{alertblock}{Theorem (Yau 1978 --- the Calabi conjecture)}

    Let $(X,\omega)$ be a compact K\"ahler manifold with $c_1(X)=0$ in
    $H^2(X,\mathbb{R})$. Then $X$ carries a \emph{unique} Ricci-flat
    K\"ahler metric $\tilde\omega$ with $[\tilde\omega]=[\omega]$:
    \[
      \operatorname{Ric}(\tilde\omega) \;=\; 0 .
    \]
    Every Calabi--Yau manifold admits Ricci-flat metrics --- exactly one in
    each K\"ahler class.

    \auterm{How is it proved?}\ Fix an overall scale; every candidate metric
    is then $\omega + i\,\partial\bar\partial\varphi$ for a single function
    $\varphi$, and Ricci-flatness becomes one nonlinear PDE --- the
    \emph{complex Monge--Amp\`ere equation}
    \[
      \bigl(\omega + i\,\partial\bar\partial\varphi\bigr)^{\,n}
      \;=\; e^{F}\,\omega^{n}
    \]
    --- whose unknown sits inside a determinant of its own second
    derivatives. Yau's a~priori estimates force a solution to exist.

  \end{alertblock}

  \begin{block}{The simplest one: a donut}

    In one complex dimension the definition selects exactly one shape: the
    torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$, the surface of a donut. Its volume form is
    $dz$, inherited from the plane, and its Ricci-flat metric is the flat
    geometry a sheet of paper keeps when rolled up in two directions.

    \begin{center}
      \includegraphics[width=0.22\colwidth]{figures/cy-torus.png}
    \end{center}

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{The family album}

    One dimension up sit the celebrated \auterm{K3 surfaces}; in dimension
    three, the \auterm{quintic}: one degree-$d$ equation in complex
    projective space $\mathbb{P}^{n+1}$ cuts out a Calabi--Yau exactly when
    $d = n+2$.

  \end{block}

  \begin{exampleblock}{Why physicists care}

    String theory needs spacetime to have \textbf{ten} dimensions --- four we
    see, six curled up too small to notice. In 1985 Candelas, Horowitz,
    Strominger and Witten showed that when the hidden six form a Calabi--Yau
    threefold, the four-dimensional physics that remains keeps supersymmetry.

    \auterm{A shape and its mirror.}\ Calabi--Yau threefolds come in pairs:
    each $X$ has a \emph{mirror} $X^{\vee}$ with its two ``shape numbers''
    swapped, yet string theory cannot tell the pair apart. In 1991
    Candelas--de~la~Ossa--Green--Parkes used this to count rational curves
    on the quintic ($2875$ lines, \dots) by an integral on the mirror.

  \end{exampleblock}
