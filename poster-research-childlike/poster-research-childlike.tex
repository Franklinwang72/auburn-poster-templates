% ---------- LEFT: the prerequisites, built up for an undergraduate ----------
\PosterSection{What Is a Manifold?}
A \auterm{manifold} is a mathematician's word for a smooth shape --- of
any dimension. Its one defining trick: up close it looks \auterm{flat},
the way the ground looks flat from where you stand even though the
Earth is round. A circle, the skin of a doughnut, the fabric of
spacetime --- all are manifolds. The number of coordinates you need to
pin down a point is the \auterm{dimension}, and the shapes here are
\auterm{compact}: finite and edgeless, closed up like a sphere rather
than sprawling like an open plain.
\vfill
\PosterSection{Complex Dimensions}
Calabi--Yau manifolds run on \auterm{complex} coordinates. A complex
number $x + iy$ is a single arrow in the plane --- length and
direction bundled into one --- so one complex dimension already
carries two real ones. An $n$-complex-dimensional shape is therefore
$2n$-real-dimensional: a Calabi--Yau \auterm{threefold} $(n = 3)$ is a
\auterm{six}-dimensional world. Complex coordinates also unlock the
rigid, powerful toolbox of complex analysis.
\vfill
\PosterSection{The Family Album}
One dimension up sit the celebrated \auterm{K3 surfaces}; in dimension
three, the \auterm{quintic}. A shape cut out by one degree-$d$
equation in complex projective space $\mathbb{P}^{n+1}$ is
Calabi--Yau exactly when $d = n+2$:
\begin{center}

\end{center}
\vspace{0.05em}
The quintic alone moves in a $101$-parameter family of Ricci-flat
worlds.
\vfill
\PosterSection{Curvature: Do Triangles Behave?}
How can you tell a surface is curved without stepping outside it? Draw
a triangle. On a \auterm{sphere} its angles sum to \auterm{more} than
$180^\circ$; on a \auterm{saddle}, to \auterm{less}; only on a
\auterm{flat} sheet do they hit $180^\circ$ exactly. An ant that never
leaves the surface could discover its world is round this way ---
curvature is \auterm{intrinsic}, and it can change from point to point.
\begin{center}

  three outward-bulging arcs, a flat gray sheet with a straight-edged triangle,
  and an orange saddle surface with a triangle whose sides pinch inward.}{%
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{figures/curvature-trio.png}}
\par\vspace{0.1em}
{\PosterCaption\color{InkSoft}
Sphere: more than $180^\circ$ \textperiodcentered\;
flat sheet: exactly $180^\circ$ \textperiodcentered\;
saddle: less than $180^\circ$.\par}
\end{center}
\vfill
\PosterSection{Ricci Curvature}
Past two dimensions, curvature splits into many independent pieces.
The \auterm{Ricci curvature} is the piece that controls \auterm{volume}.
Sprinkle a tiny ball of dust and let each speck drift along a straight
line: on a sphere the ball shrinks, on a saddle it swells, and when
$\operatorname{Ric} = 0$ it holds its volume exactly. Such a space is
genuinely curved yet volume-balanced --- which is precisely Einstein's
equation for a spacetime with no matter. \auterm{Ricci-flat spaces are
gravity's vacuums.}

\end{minipage}\hfill

\PosterSection{What Is a Calabi--Yau Manifold?}
A \auterm{Calabi--Yau manifold} is a compact complex manifold with two
features. \auterm{(1) K\"ahler:} it carries both a way to measure
distance --- a metric --- and complex coordinates, fitting together as
smoothly as possible. \auterm{(2) A holomorphic volume form} $\Omega$:
a way to gauge complex volume, built from the coordinates, that
vanishes {\Serif\itshape nowhere} (in bundle language, a trivial canonical
bundle $K_X \cong \mathcal{O}_X$). Feature (2) forces a purely
\auterm{topological} quantity to vanish --- the \auterm{first Chern
class} $c_1(X)$, a single whole-number invariant of the shape you can
compute without measuring anything. Calabi asked whether that cheap
test forces a Ricci-flat metric to exist.
\par\vspace{0.2em}
{\setlength{\fboxrule}{1.6pt}\setlength{\fboxsep}{0pt}%
\fcolorbox{SketchBorder}{SkyWash}{\begin{minipage}{\dimexpr\textwidth-3.2pt}
  \vspace{0.65em}\begin{center}\begin{minipage}{\dimexpr\textwidth-3.4em}
  {\Serif\itshape\fontsize{24}{30}\selectfont\color{AUorange}%
   Theorem\ (Yau 1978 --- the Calabi conjecture)\par}
  \vspace{0.35em}
  {\Serif\fontsize{27.5}{38}\selectfont\color{AUnavy}
  Let $(X,\omega)$ be a compact K\"ahler manifold with
  $c_1(X) = 0$ in $H^2(X,\mathbb{R})$. Then $X$ carries a
  \textit{unique} Ricci-flat K\"ahler metric $\tilde\omega$ with
  $[\tilde\omega] = [\omega]$:
  {\fontsize{35}{41}\selectfont
  \begin{equation*}
    \operatorname{Ric}(\tilde\omega) \;=\; 0 .
  \end{equation*}}
  Every Calabi--Yau manifold admits Ricci-flat metrics ---
  exactly one in each K\"ahler class.\par}
  \end{minipage}\end{center}\vspace{0.65em}
\end{minipage}}}
\vfill
\auterm{How is it proved?}\ Fix an overall scale; every candidate
metric is then $\omega + i\,\partial\bar\partial\varphi$ for a single
function $\varphi$, and Ricci-flatness becomes one nonlinear PDE ---
the \auterm{complex Monge--Amp\`ere equation}
{\fontsize{26}{31}\selectfont
\begin{equation*}
  \bigl(\omega + i\,\partial\bar\partial\varphi\bigr)^{\,n}
  \;=\; e^{F}\,\omega^{n}
\end{equation*}}
--- whose unknown sits inside a determinant of its own second
derivatives. Yau's a~priori estimates force a solution to exist.
\vfill

\PosterSection{The Simplest One: A Donut}
In one complex dimension the definition selects exactly one shape: the
torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$, the surface of a donut. Its volume form is
$dz$, inherited from the plane, and its Ricci-flat metric is the flat
geometry a sheet of paper keeps when rolled up in two directions ---
everything Yau's theorem promises, visible by hand.
\begin{center}
\PosterFigure{A glossy navy torus, the surface of a donut, with one
  vertical circle around the tube and one horizontal circle around
  the hole drawn in deep navy: the two directions in which the flat
  sheet was rolled up.}{%
  \includegraphics[width=0.28\textwidth]{figures/cy-torus.png}}
\par\vspace{0.1em}
{\PosterCaption\color{InkSoft}
The torus: a flat sheet rolled up in two directions.\par}
\end{center}
\vfill
\PosterSection{Six Hidden Dimensions}
String theory needs spacetime to have \auterm{ten} dimensions --- four
we see, six curled up too small to notice. In 1985 Candelas, Horowitz,
Strominger and Witten showed that when the hidden six form a
Calabi--Yau threefold, the physics left in four dimensions keeps
supersymmetry, with Yau's Ricci-flat metric as its vacuum. If string
theory is right, a shape like the one at the centre of this poster
sits at every point of space.
\vfill
\PosterSection{A Shape and Its Mirror}
Calabi--Yau threefolds come in pairs: each $X$ has a \auterm{mirror}
$X^{\vee}$ with its two ``shape numbers'' swapped --- one counts ways
to resize the shape, the other ways to deform it. String theory cannot
tell the pair apart, and in 1991 Candelas--de~la~Ossa--Green--Parkes
used this to count rational curves on the quintic ($2875$ lines,
$609{,}250$ conics) by an integral on the mirror.
\vfill
\PosterSection{Takeaway}
{\raggedright\hyphenpenalty=10000\exhyphenpenalty=10000
A Calabi--Yau manifold passes one topological test, $c_1 = 0$ ---
and Yau's theorem rewards it with perfect geometry,
$\operatorname{Ric} = 0$: the meeting point of analysis, algebra, and
physics.\par}
